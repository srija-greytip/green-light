# **Synthetic Datasets Reference**

Three CSVs in `data/synthetic/`, generated by `scripts/generate_data.py` (seeded, reproducible).

> **What these can and cannot show.** The rejection rates below are ones *I authored* — they validate that the pipeline and scorer behave correctly on known patterns. They are **not** evidence about real-world approval behaviour, and no threshold recommendation should ever be drawn from them.

---

**Strict at MBU** — Highly conservative policy. Only claims that follow the employee’s historical precedent are approved. Any deviation or unfamiliar claim is automatically rejected, making approvals rare and more meaningful indicators of "in-pattern" spending.

**Regular at SBU** — Balanced policy. Standard, in-pattern claims are approved routinely. Out-of-pattern claims are considered, sometimes rejected, or accepted after further review, reflecting realistic but attentive scrutiny from reviewers.

**Lenient at LBU** — Permissive policy. The majority of claims are approved with minimal regard for whether they match the claimant’s history. Approvals are frequent, with little discrimination, so acceptance says little about the validity of a claim.

**Strict at LBU** — Conservative policy at the LBU level, mirroring the strict behavior at MBU. Only claims matching employee precedent are approved; any variation leads to rejection, ensuring approvals are strong signals of conformity.

**Regular at MBU** — Moderately attentive policy at MBU, similar to regular at SBU. In-pattern claims are approved smoothly, while unusual claims undergo genuine review and mixed outcomes, maintaining a careful but not severe approach.

**Lenient at SBU** — Highly permissive approach at SBU. Most claims are approved regardless of their alignment with claimant history, echoing the lenient pattern at LBU, with approvals having low informational value about claim sensibility.

## **5\. What differs between the three datasets**

Same employees, same categories, same archetypes, same amount distributions. **Only the outcome-decision function changes** — specifically how steeply rejection probability rises as a claim deviates from that person's own established pattern.

```
P(reject) = base + slope × deviation^exponent
```

|  | base | slope | exponent | Effect |
| ----- | ----- | ----- | ----- | ----- |
| **lenient** | 0.004 | 0.13 | 3.0 | near-flat — deviation barely matters |
| **regular** | 0.020 | 0.62 | 1.9 | moderate |
| **strict** | 0.030 | 0.95 | 1.25 | steep — even mild deviation is punished |

### **What each dataset is for**

* **regular** — the realistic middle. Threshold sweeps and the main demo run here.  
* **strict** — deviation is strongly predictive of rejection. The scorer *should* separate cleanly; if it can't, the scorer is weak.  
* **lenient** — approval carries almost no information. The scorer **should be unable to discriminate**. If it appears to, that's a bug or a leak. This is a genuinely useful negative control that real data can't give you.

### **The grain finding**

Profiling at **category** level blends types that differ by 50×. **cheap types get their anomalies masked by expensive types sharing the category.**

A claim at 2× the person's own norm for that type:

| Category | Type | z @ type | z @ category |  |
| ----- | ----- | ----- | ----- | ----- |
| MED | Consultation | **20.0** | **1.3** | **masked** |
| INT | Mobile Postpaid | **3.2** | **0.9** | **masked** |
| TRVL | Local Cab | 2.9 | 0.8 | nearly masked |
| MEAL | Team Dinner | 9.8 | 106.5 | fine |
| TRVL | Airfare | 2.2 | 140.3 | fine |

**"Masked"** just means: the category-level view hid something the type-level view caught. 

A doubled Consultation is a 20-sigma event against that person's consultation history — and completely unremarkable against the Medical category, because Hospitalisation stretches that band to ₹35k.

**The plain conclusion:** if the scorer profiles people at the *category* level (Medical as one bucket), a doubled consultation bill can slip through unnoticed, hiding behind expensive hospital claims that happen to share the same category. Profile at the *type* level instead (Consultation on its own), and it gets caught.

**The cost:** type-level means smaller groups to build a pattern from — 97 pattern-groups instead of 80 — so there's a bit less history to work with per group. But given it directly prevents a real claim from slipping through unnoticed, that trade looks worth it.

**This argues for `PROFILE_GRAIN = "type"`.** It also costs something: type-level gives 97 pairs vs 80 at category level, so precedent is thinner. Worth measuring both, but the masking effect is concrete evidence, not speculation.

## **8\. Known limitations**

1. **Rejection rates are authored, not observed.** No calibration conclusion can come from these.  
2. **Archetype labels are not in the CSV.** They're generator-internal, so nothing can accidentally train on them. Re-derivable from `scripts/generate_data.py` if needed for diagnosis.  
3. **`MED` is thin** — 29 claims across 10 employees. Deliberate (it's the rare one-off case), but too thin for per-category threshold tuning.  
4. **Peer/role features remain impossible** — no role, grade, department or location exists in the source schema, so none is generated.
