# **Synthetic Datasets Reference**

Three CSVs in `data/synthetic/`, generated by `scripts/generate_data.py` (seeded, reproducible).

> **What these can and cannot show.** The rejection rates below are ones *I authored* — they validate that the pipeline and scorer behave correctly on known patterns. They are **not** evidence about real-world approval behaviour, and no threshold recommendation should ever be drawn from them.

---

**Strict at MBU** — Highly conservative policy. Only claims that follow the employee’s historical precedent are approved. Any deviation or unfamiliar claim is automatically rejected, making approvals rare and more meaningful indicators of "in-pattern" spending.

**Regular at SBU** — Balanced policy. Standard, in-pattern claims are approved routinely. Out-of-pattern claims are considered, sometimes rejected, or accepted after further review, reflecting realistic but attentive scrutiny from reviewers.

**Lenient at LBU** — Permissive policy. The majority of claims are approved with minimal regard for whether they match the claimant’s history. Approvals are frequent, with little discrimination, so acceptance says little about the validity of a claim.

**Strict at LBU** — Conservative policy at the LBU level, mirroring the strict behavior at MBU. Only claims matching employee precedent are approved; any variation leads to rejection, ensuring approvals are strong signals of conformity.

**Regular at MBU** — Moderately attentive policy at MBU, similar to regular at SBU. In-pattern claims are approved smoothly, while unusual claims undergo genuine review and mixed outcomes, maintaining a careful but not severe approach.

**Lenient at SBU** — Highly permissive approach at SBU. Most claims are approved regardless of their alignment with claimant history, echoing the lenient pattern at LBU, with approvals having low informational value about claim sensibility.

**Type coverage is not guaranteed at small BU sizes.** With only 10 employees, random archetype assignment doesn't always touch every type: `regular_sbu` exercises 12 of 15 types, `lenient_sbu` 14 of 15. ## 4. What differs between the six datasets
 
Two independent variables, not one: **policy** (how sharply rejection tracks deviation from the employee's own pattern) and **company size** (how much precedent accumulates). Same archetype machinery, same categories, across all six.
 
```
P(reject) = base + slope × deviation^exponent
```
 
| Policy | base | slope | exponent | Effect |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---|
| **lenient** | 0.004 | 0.13 | 3.0 | near-flat — deviation barely matters |
| **regular** | 0.020 | 0.62 | 1.9 | moderate |
| **strict** | 0.030 | 0.95 | 1.25 | steep — even mild deviation is punished |

### Rejection rate by category, all six
 
| Category | strict_mbu | regular_sbu | lenient_lbu | strict_lbu | regular_mbu | lenient_sbu |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| FUEL | 29.0% | 9.8% | 2.0% | 25.5% | 10.7% | 3.8% |
| INT | 34.2% | 9.1% | 1.4% | 24.6% | 12.2% | 0.0% |
| LEARN | 26.9% | 12.5% | 1.2% | 27.0% | 18.9% | 0.0% |
| MEAL | 24.7% | 8.8% | 0.9% | 24.3% | 8.6% | 0.6% |
| MED | 22.6% | 12.5% | 3.3% | 34.1% | 8.5% | 0.0% |
| TRVL | 18.2% | 27.3% | 1.6% | 22.6% | 9.7% | 1.7% |
 
Policy separation (strict ≫ regular ≫ lenient) holds **within every category**, at every company size — the mechanism isn't an artifact of aggregation.

### What each is for
 
- **strict_mbu / strict_lbu** — deviation strongly predicts rejection. The scorer should separate cleanly at both sizes; comparing the two isolates whether more precedent (LBU) sharpens that separation further.
- **regular_sbu / regular_mbu** — the realistic middle, at two scales. `regular_sbu` is the harder case — half the employees, thinner precedent.
- **lenient_lbu / lenient_sbu** — negative controls. Approval carries almost no information; the scorer **should be unable to discriminate**. If it appears to, that's a bug or a leak. `lenient_sbu` is the hardest version of this test — thinnest precedent *and* weakest signal.

| Dataset | Type-level pairs | Eligible | Category-level pairs | Eligible |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| strict_mbu | 87 | 56 (64%) | 70 | 52 (74%) |
| regular_sbu | 39 | 28 (72%) | 30 | 21 (70%) |
| lenient_lbu | 192 | 124 (65%) | 160 | 113 (71%) |
| strict_lbu | 162 | 104 (64%) | 134 | 98 (73%) |
| regular_mbu | 96 | 65 (68%) | 82 | 61 (74%) |
| lenient_sbu | 38 | 23 (61%) | 30 | 21 (70%) |
 
Eligibility rate holds in a tight 61–74% band regardless of company size — SBU has far fewer *pairs* in absolute terms, but not a worse *fraction* of them being scoreable. Absolute precedent still shrinks with company size, though: SBU has roughly a third of MBU's eligible pairs, which is the real cost of a smaller customer.
 
### The grain finding, re-verified on this generation
 
A claim at 2× the person's own norm for that type (regular_mbu):
 
| Category | Type | n | z @ type | z @ category | |
|---|---|---:|---:|---:|---|
| MED | Hospitalisation | 9 | 5.4 | 186.9 | fine |
| INT | **Mobile Postpaid** | 61 | **4.2** | **2.1** | **masked** |
| TRVL | Airfare | 27 | 3.4 | 100.4 | fine |
| TRVL | Local Cab | 139 | 2.7 | 0.9 | nearly masked |
| MED | Consultation | 31 | 2.9 | 1.8 | nearly masked |
 
Same effect as the earlier three-dataset run, though the specific masked type differs run to run (random draws), which is itself informative: **it's not one fragile example, it's a reproducible property of blending types with very different amount scales — it shows up under different seeds, different company sizes, different policies.** Mobile Postpaid hiding behind Broadband in the same category is the concrete case here; a doubled Mobile Postpaid bill is a real anomaly against that person's own history (z=4.2) and nearly invisible against the whole Internet & Telecom category (z=2.1).
 
**This still argues for `PROFILE_GRAIN = "type"`.**

## 7. Known limitations
 
1. **Rejection rates are authored, not observed.** No calibration conclusion can come from these.
2. **Archetype labels are not in the CSV.** Generator-internal, so nothing can accidentally train on them. Re-derivable from `scripts/generate_six_datasets.py` for diagnosis.
3. **`MED` is thin everywhere**, most severely at SBU. Deliberate — it's the rare one-off case — but too thin for per-category threshold tuning at small company sizes.
4. **SBU doesn't guarantee full type coverage** (§1) — a real property of small companies, not a generation bug, but worth remembering when comparing SBU results against MBU/LBU.
5. **Auto-approval rate is uncontrolled across datasets** (§2) — accepted as harmless since the column is exclusion-only, never a feature.
6. **Peer/role features remain impossible** — no role, grade, department or location exists in the source schema, so none is generated.
